A Black and White NFL Divide

How Football Fans' Tweets Support Theories of Racial Differences in Language

Posted by Julianna Alvord on December 4, 2018

I’ve been watching football my whole life. My dad has always been a Giants fan, growing up in New Jersey. I spent a lot of my childhood sitting with him and watching Eli Manning and the rest of the team play. From their two Super Bowl upsets in 2008 and 2012 to lazy Sunday afternoons, I associate many positive memories with watching football. It has become obvious to me now that I was watching through rose-colored glasses.

My first experience questioning the sport of football and the NFL as an establishment came after watching the movie Concussion, in 2015. The movie tells the true story of Dr. Omalu, a forensic neuropathologist who made the first discovery of CTE, a football-related brain trauma, and fought to the NFL to acknowledge its existence. Though shocked and saddened by the story at the time, I noticed the NFL actively taking steps towards reducing head injuries through contact restrictions and fines. I was naive to look the other way and assume the problem had been taken care of.

It wasn’t until I got to college and took a class titled Body Images & Sports Media where I truly became wary of the issues surrounding sports media and in relation to football especially.
